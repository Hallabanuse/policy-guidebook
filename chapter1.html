Chapter 1: Understanding Policy Processes

Leave Feedback

Conservation does not happen only in forests, rivers, or protected areas. It also unfolds in meeting rooms, legislative chambers, and public consultations—spaces where decisions shape what protection is possible, funded, or enforced. For many practitioners, meaningful conservation therefore requires stepping beyond on-the-ground action and into the complex world of policy.

This chapter provides the foundation for that step. It explains how policy processes work, why they often feel unpredictable, and what challenges you will face when trying to influence them. Understanding these dynamics is essential before picking up the practical tools in Chapters 2, 3, and 4.

Key Takeaways

  • Policy processes are messy and unpredictable. Policies move through stages—from agenda-setting to formulation, adoption, implementation, and review. But getting an issue onto the agenda in the first place is the hard part. The Multiple Streams Framework explains how this happens: problems, politics, and viable solutions must converge in brief windows of opportunity.
  • Policy windows are critical moments. Most major policy changes happen when the right problem, the right political conditions, and a viable solution come together. These windows are often short and unpredictable, which means preparation matters as much as timing.
  • Success is hard to define and harder to claim. What counts as policy success? A law passed? A commitment made? Real implementation? Even when outcomes are clear, multiple actors contribute to every result. Attribution to any single organisation is inherently difficult.
  • Evidence alone doesn't change minds. Scientific data matters enormously for credibility and for making the case once decision-makers are receptive—but the assumption that good evidence leads directly to good policy is wrong. Evidence is necessary but not sufficient.
  • Policy happens at multiple levels. Advocacy occurs at global, regional, national, and local levels simultaneously. International commitments can create pressure and reference points, but real change requires national and local action.

1.1 How Policy Processes Work

Understanding how policies are created is essential for those who seek to influence them. This section introduces the policy cycle—the stages that policies move through—and then focuses on agenda-setting, often the most critical and unpredictable stage. The Multiple Streams Framework helps explain why some issues gain political attention while others are ignored, and what it takes for change to happen.

The policy cycle

Policy processes differ across global, regional, national, and local systems, but they often follow a similar pattern. The policy cycle is a common way to describe this pattern, depicting policymaking as a series of stages:

  1. Agenda-setting: Decision-makers identify and prioritise issues. This process is shaped by how problems are defined, political receptivity, and what solutions are available.
  2. Policy formulation: Policies are drafted, often involving multiple rounds of consultation, research, and negotiation.
  3. Adoption: The policy is formally approved by the relevant authorities. The procedural steps vary across political systems.
  4. Implementation: Adopted policies are translated into action through programmes, regulations, and administrative procedures.
  5. Review and evaluation: The policy's implementation and effectiveness are assessed. Ideally, this feeds back into agenda-setting, and the cycle continues.

The cycle is often depicted as a circle to highlight its iterative nature. In reality, however, policymaking is rarely this linear. Stages overlap, reverse, or skip entirely. A policy may be adopted but never implemented. An issue may cycle through agenda-setting repeatedly without ever reaching adoption. The cycle is useful as a simplified model, but it does not capture the messy reality of how policy actually happens.

What shapes advocacy success

Policy engagement and the success of advocacy are shaped by three main factors:

General constraints are the systemic factors that shape civil society's ability to influence the policymaking process: the transparency of institutions, access to decision-makers, availability of funding, inclusive consultation mechanisms, and rules of procedure. These constraints can either limit or facilitate engagement. In some contexts, overcoming them is a strategic focus in itself.

Capacity to deliver refers to an organisation's or coalition's ability to engage effectively. This includes skills, knowledge, relationships, credibility, and resources. Strategies to enhance capacity include forming coalitions, internal capacity-building, and securing funding.

Policy windows are moments of opportunity when the conditions for policy change align. Their presence or absence significantly influences the effectiveness of engagement strategies. Understanding when and how windows open—and being prepared when they do—is essential for effective advocacy.

The rest of this section focuses on policy windows, as they are often the most critical and least understood factor.

Policy windows

Why do some issues gain political attention while others are ignored for years? Why does change sometimes happen suddenly after long periods of stagnation? The concept of policy windows helps explain these dynamics.

A policy window is a moment when the conditions for policy change align—when advocates can push their issues onto the agenda and see action taken. John Kingdon's Multiple Streams Framework explains how these windows emerge through the interaction of three streams:

The problems stream: Various conditions compete for attention. A condition becomes a recognised problem when people believe something should be done about it. Problems gain attention through systematic indicators (data showing a condition is worsening), focusing events (crises, disasters, or scandals that shift attention), or feedback on existing policies (evidence that current approaches are failing).

The political stream: This stream follows its own dynamics—elections, shifts in public mood, changes in government, and pressure from interest groups. What happens here strongly affects which issues rise on the agenda. A new administration may bring entirely different priorities. A shift in public opinion may make previously ignored issues suddenly urgent.

The policy stream: Ideas, proposals, and solutions float around in what Kingdon calls a "primeval soup." Specialists develop and refine proposals, test them in various forums, and build support. Not all ideas survive—they are filtered by criteria such as technical feasibility, alignment with values, and anticipated political acceptability. The ideas that survive this process are ready to be picked up when an opportunity arises.

In practice, these streams are interconnected—but thinking about them separately makes it easier to analyse what is happening and where to focus. A problem may be recognised, but no viable solution exists. A solution may be ready, but there is no political appetite for it. Political conditions may be favourable, but no one has framed the issue effectively.

The Multiple Streams Framework was developed to explain agenda-setting in democratic systems, where public opinion influences elections and civil society can access decision-makers. In other political contexts, the dynamics may differ significantly. See the Introduction for a note on the scope of this guidebook.

When do windows open?

Policy windows can be predictable or unpredictable:

  • Predictable windows arise from scheduled events: the renewal of a law, an upcoming election, a Conference of the Parties, or a required policy review. Advocates can prepare for these in advance.
  • Unpredictable windows arise from crises, disasters, scandals, or unexpected political changes. These require the capacity to respond quickly.

Windows can also be opened by events in different streams:

  • A problem window opens when a new problem suddenly demands attention—a disaster, a damning report, a focusing event. Decision-makers search for solutions.
  • A political window opens when political conditions shift—a new government takes office, public opinion changes, or an election creates pressure to act. Politicians look for problems to solve and commitments to make.

"You need this window of opportunity. And then change can happen really fast. This is something that keeps me going. Sometimes you think it will never change, and then suddenly everything comes together."

– Ulrike Kallee

Coupling: linking problems, politics, and solutions

When a window opens, coupling becomes possible: linking a recognised problem to a viable solution in a favourable political moment. This coupling does not happen automatically. It requires advocates who have done the preparation—who have solutions ready, relationships in place, and the ability to frame issues in ways that resonate with the political moment.

Partial couplings happen frequently: a problem is recognised but no solution is ready; a solution exists but there is no political appetite; politics and problems align but no one has a workable proposal. These partial couplings may build pressure over time, but they rarely produce immediate action. It is only when all three streams converge that the likelihood of policy change significantly increases.

Case Study: Protected Areas in Guatemala

In the mid-1980s, Guatemala stood at a crossroads. After sixteen years of military rule, the country transitioned to civilian governance with the election of President Vinicio Cerezo in 1986. Only 2.5% of Guatemala's territory was protected, and forest cover had dropped from 68% to 39% in two decades.

The political window: The transition to democracy opened a window for policy change. A new constitution (1985) enabled democratic policy-making processes, including the regulation of environmental issues. Conservation remained non-controversial in Guatemalan politics, with weak and fragmented opposition from industries and landowners.

The problems stream: Rapid deforestation and environmental degradation were increasingly visible. Population growth and migration into forested areas placed pressure on remaining natural resources.

The political stream: Key conservation leaders had personal ties to influential political actors—Marco Cerezo (son of the President) and Jorge Cabrera (brother of the President of the Parliament). Globally, biodiversity conservation was gaining momentum, with IUCN pushing for ambitious protection targets.

The policy stream: National NGOs like Defensores de la Naturaleza had spent years preparing. They conducted biodiversity surveys, identified critical areas, and developed legislative proposals. International partners (including The Nature Conservancy) provided funding and technical expertise.

Coupling: When the window opened, advocates were ready. They had solutions prepared, relationships in place, and framing that resonated with the political moment.

The outcome: In 1989, Guatemala passed the Protected Areas Law, establishing 42 protected areas covering nearly 25% of the country's territory. The Sierra de las Minas Biosphere Reserve became the first protected area in Latin America with management formally delegated to an NGO.

Source: Garrelts, Birner & Wittmer (2005)

Windows can also close quickly. Action may be taken (successfully or not), and attention moves elsewhere. The political moment passes. Other issues crowd onto the agenda. The window that seemed open yesterday may be closed today.

Chapter 3 explores strategies for preparing for and responding to policy windows: foreseeing opportunities, building coalitions, framing issues effectively, and persevering when windows are closed.

1.2 Key Challenges in Policy Engagement

Understanding how policy works is only the first step. This section highlights three challenges that shape what is possible—and realistic—when engaging with policy processes. Recognising these challenges upfront helps set appropriate expectations and informs how you plan, act, and learn.

Success is hard to define and harder to claim

Policy engagement does not produce simple, measurable outcomes like a protected area boundary or a species count. Success in policy is layered, contested, and often ambiguous.

What counts as success? Consider the possibilities:

  • Getting an issue onto the political agenda
  • Securing a public commitment from decision-makers
  • Having language included in a policy document
  • Getting a law passed
  • Seeing that law funded and implemented
  • Observing changes in behaviour on the ground
  • Measuring impact on biodiversity or human wellbeing

Each of these represents a different level of success, and achieving one does not guarantee the next. A law can pass but remain unfunded. A commitment can be made but not honoured. Implementation can begin but stall.

Did success even happen? Policy change is often incremental, partial, or reversible. A provision gets weakened during negotiation. Implementation varies across jurisdictions. A new government reverses the policy entirely. What looked like success at one moment may not look like success later.

Did you contribute? Even when success is clear, many actors are involved in any policy outcome. NGOs, researchers, government champions, international pressure, media coverage, public opinion, and sheer timing all play roles. Attributing the outcome to any single organisation's efforts is inherently difficult—and often impossible.

Would it have happened anyway? The counterfactual problem haunts all policy evaluation. Perhaps the window would have opened regardless. Perhaps another actor would have filled your role. This uncertainty is not a reason to disengage, but it does shape how we think about measuring success.

These challenges do not mean success cannot be assessed. Chapter 4 introduces approaches—such as outcome harvesting and contribution analysis—that help teams document what changed, understand their role, and learn for the future. The key is to plan for contribution, not control, and to build learning into your approach from the start.

Evidence alone doesn't change minds

Many conservation organisations engage where science and policy meet, advocating for the use of data to improve environmental outcomes. The assumption is intuitive: if decision-makers understood the evidence, they would make better choices.

In practice, this assumption is often wrong.

Political priorities are shaped by many factors: public opinion, electoral calculations, personal motivations, interest group pressure, and competing demands on attention. Evidence is one input among many, and it is rarely the decisive one. Decision-makers may have all the data and still not act—because the politics are not right, because other issues take priority, because acting would conflict with other goals.

"If the world as a whole followed scientific data, we would not continue heating up the planet and destroying biodiversity. So pure scientific evidence does not ultimately or directly lead to better environmental policies."

– Stefan Ferger

This does not mean evidence is unimportant. It means its role is more specific than often assumed:

  • Evidence is essential for credibility. Walking into a meeting without supporting data can be counterproductive. Being able to back up your arguments makes them harder to dismiss.
  • Evidence matters most when windows are open. Once decision-makers are receptive and actively seeking solutions, evidence becomes highly valuable. It helps define problems, justify solutions, and demonstrate feasibility.
  • Evidence is critical for implementation. After a policy is adopted, technical expertise is needed for rule-making, guidance, and ensuring the original intent is carried out.
  • The messenger matters as much as the message. Who presents the evidence affects how it is received. Trusted voices, neutral parties, and community members may be more effective than advocacy organisations perceived as partial.

The challenge for practitioners is to use evidence strategically—understanding when it can make a difference and when other factors will dominate. Chapter 3 includes guidance on framing and timing evidence use as part of your engagement strategy.

Policy happens at multiple levels

Environmental issues do not respect jurisdictional boundaries. Migratory species cross borders. Climate change is global. Supply chains span continents. This means policy engagement often involves multiple levels of governance simultaneously.

Global and regional levels: International agreements—such as the Convention on Biological Diversity or UNFCCC—set frameworks, establish commitments, and create pressure for national action. They provide reference points that civil society can use to hold governments accountable. High-profile conferences generate media attention and political momentum.

National level: This is where most binding law is made and where enforcement is possible. National politics, institutions, and capacity determine whether international commitments translate into action. A global target means little if national legislation does not follow.

Local level: Implementation happens on the ground. Local governments, communities, and land managers make daily decisions that determine whether policies achieve their intended impact.

These levels interact in complex ways. International commitments can create pressure for national action. National frameworks can enable or constrain local implementation. Local experience can inform national policy and feed into international negotiations.

For practitioners, this means thinking strategically about where to engage:

  • Where is the decision actually made?
  • Where is political receptivity highest?
  • Where do you have relationships and credibility?
  • How do actions at one level create pressure or opportunity at another?

1.3 Why You Need Structured Tools

The picture painted in this chapter may seem daunting. Policy processes are messy and unpredictable. Windows are brief and hard to anticipate. Success is ambiguous and hard to claim. Evidence alone is not enough. Multiple levels interact in complex ways.

But these realities are not reasons for despair—they are reasons for preparation.

If policy processes were predictable, you could wing it. Because they are not, you need to understand your context before you act. This is what Chapter 2 provides: tools for assessing the policy landscape, mapping stakeholders, and understanding where you are in the three streams.

If you cannot control outcomes, you need to be strategic about where you focus. This is what Chapter 3 provides: approaches for setting goals, selecting strategies, building theories of change, and identifying enablers and barriers.

If success is hard to measure, you need methods designed for complexity. This is what Chapter 4 provides: approaches like outcome harvesting and contribution analysis that help you learn from experience even when attribution is difficult.

The rest of this guidebook is practical. It provides steps, tools, and worked examples—including a case study that runs through Chapters 2, 3, and 4. But the foundation is here: an understanding of how policy works and what makes it hard. With that foundation in place, the tools become meaningful.
