Chapter 2: Assessing the Policy Context

Before engaging in advocacy, you need to understand your context. What is the state of the problem you want to address? Who are the key decision-makers and what drives their priorities? What policy solutions already exist or are being developed? And critically: is your team positioned to engage effectively?

This chapter provides tools for answering these questions. It introduces a practical approach to policy context assessment based on the three streams described in Chapter 1—problems, politics, and policies. The Policy Context Tool helps you systematically analyse these streams to identify where conditions are favourable, where barriers exist, and when policy windows might open.

Starting points

Teams come to policy engagement through different paths:

  • A conservation team using the Conservation Standards may identify "inadequate regulatory framework" or "weak enforcement" as a contributing factor in their situation model—and realise they need to understand the political landscape to address it.
  • A policy team may spot a political opportunity—an upcoming election, a scheduled policy review—or be responding to a regulatory threat that affects their work.

Whatever the entry point, the challenge is the same: understanding the political context well enough to act strategically. Recognising that policy matters is not the same as knowing how to engage effectively. What are the conditions that would make policy change possible? Is the problem recognised by decision-makers? Are there political actors who might champion this cause? Do feasible policy solutions exist? When might a window of opportunity open?

The Policy Context Tool introduced in this chapter helps answer these questions. It provides a structured way to assess the political environment—building on the three streams framework from Chapter 1—so that teams can identify where conditions are favourable, where barriers exist, and when windows might open.

Key Takeaways

  • Start with your own readiness. Before assessing the external landscape, reflect on your team's capacity, expertise, relationships, and credibility. Not every organisation is well-positioned for every advocacy opportunity.
  • Coalitions can fill gaps. If your team lacks certain capacities, partnerships can help. Coalitions pool resources, increase credibility, and extend reach to decision-makers you cannot access alone.
  • Use the three streams as your framework. The problems, political, and policy streams provide a structured way to assess the context. Various tools—stakeholder analysis, horizon scanning, legal framework analysis—can help you dig deeper into each stream.
  • Assessment informs strategy. The purpose of assessment is not to produce a report—it is to understand your context well enough to act strategically. Assessment should lead directly to planning (Chapter 3).

2.1 Your Team and Readiness

Before diving into external analysis, take stock of your own organisation. Advocacy requires specific skills, relationships, and resources that may differ from those needed for other conservation work.

"Ask yourself, are we as an organisation, with the specific skills we have, the best candidate to address this issue?"

– Sascha Müller-Kraenner

Internal capacity

An organisation's capacity for advocacy depends on factors including:

  • Expertise: Do you have staff with policy knowledge, communication skills, and understanding of the political landscape?
  • Relationships: Do you have existing connections to decision-makers, other advocacy organisations, or communities affected by the issue?
  • Credibility: Are you seen as a trustworthy voice on this issue? How do decision-makers, the public, potential partners, and industry perceive you?
  • Resources: Do you have the time, funding, and staff capacity to sustain an advocacy effort?

Capacity gaps take different forms. Conservation organisations may be staffed primarily by field staff and practitioners unfamiliar with political processes. Policy teams may have political expertise but lack the technical knowledge or field credibility needed for a specific issue. Neither is a reason to avoid engagement—but both are reasons to be realistic about what capacity-building, partnerships, or coalition work might be needed.

Which level to focus on

Policy engagement happens at multiple levels—global, regional, national, and local. As discussed in Chapter 1, these levels interact in complex ways. International commitments create pressure for national action; national frameworks shape what is possible locally; local implementation determines whether policies achieve their intended impact.

When assessing your readiness, consider which level best matches your:

  • Existing relationships: Where do you already have connections and credibility?
  • Geographic or thematic focus: Where does your organisation naturally operate?
  • Capacity: Do you have the resources and expertise for international processes, or are you better positioned for national or local engagement?
  • Theory of change: At which level is the key decision actually made? Where is change most likely to happen?

Coalitions and partnerships

Effective advocacy rarely happens in isolation. Building coalitions can help organisations:

  • Increase influence: A coordinated message from multiple organisations carries more weight than individual voices
  • Pool resources: Different organisations bring different skills, relationships, and capacities
  • Expand reach: Partners may have access to decision-makers or communities that you cannot reach alone

"You have this big stove with a lot of burners, you gotta keep a lot of things simmering all at the same time. But be strategic in which one you turn up. If it's cold, you can't use it at all. And that's where I think you go back to partnerships and coalitions—no one can do all the things all the time."

– Sarah Stachowiak

2.2 The Policy Context Tool

The Policy Context Tool provides a structured approach to understanding the political environment around a specific issue. Based on Kingdon's Multiple Streams Framework (introduced in Chapter 1), it helps teams analyse three streams—problems, politics, and policies—and identify where conditions are favourable for advocacy.

The tool can be used in multiple ways:

  • By conservation teams who have identified advocacy as a strategy in their project and need to unpack the political context around their conservation goals
  • By policy teams who work on political issues regularly and need to decide which policies and strategies to prioritise
  • Retrospectively to understand how a particular policy outcome occurred and what role policy windows played

Assessing the problems stream

In the problems stream, various conditions compete for attention. A condition becomes a recognised problem when people believe something should be done about it.

Key aspects to assess:

Focusing events: Have there been crises, disasters, studies, or developments that have drawn attention to this issue? Are any such events anticipated?

Indicators: Is there data showing the condition is worsening? Are there systematic measurements that highlight the problem?

Feedback on existing policies: Is there evidence that current approaches are failing? Have there been reviews or evaluations that highlight shortcomings?

Problem framing: How is the problem being defined by decision-makers and the media? Is it framed as a health issue, an economic issue, an environmental issue? Different framings attract different attention and suggest different solutions.

Probing questions:

  • Was there or is there any scheduled feedback on the functioning of the current legal framework?
  • Were there or do we foresee any crises, disasters, studies, or developments that change attention for the problem?
  • Is there any indication that the problem has the attention of decision-makers?
  • Is the problem framed in a certain way by decision-makers? Does the framing differ depending on the person, party, or actor?
  • How much media attention does the problem receive? How is it framed?

Evidence in the problems stream

Evidence is critical here. Data on indicators, studies documenting impacts, and evaluations of existing policies help establish whether a condition is recognised as a problem—and shape how it is framed. Without evidence, you have no case to bring to the table.

Assessing the political stream

In the political stream, decision-makers prioritise and deprioritise issues based on political context, interest group pressure, public opinion, and personal motivations.

Key aspects to assess:

Public opinion: What do different publics think about this issue? Is there support or opposition? How organised and vocal are relevant groups?

Interest groups: Who are the key sectors, industries, and interest groups that relate to this problem? What are their positions? How influential are they?

Government positions: What is the current government's stance? Who are the key decision-makers? What drives their priorities—party dynamics, electoral calculations, personal interests?

Political events: What political events are coming up (elections, government transitions, appointments) that might affect priorities or power distribution?

Stakeholder analysis

A stakeholder analysis helps map who is involved, their positions, and their influence. The most common approach involves two dimensions:

  • Influence: A person's or group's capacity to affect political agenda-setting
  • Alignment: The extent to which they share your goals

Champions are actors with high alignment and high influence—your most valuable allies. Blockers have high influence but low alignment—they can obstruct your goals and may need to be countered or worked around. Supporters share your goals but have limited influence—they may benefit from support to increase their voice. Bystanders have neither high influence nor high alignment—monitor them but don't invest heavily.

Probing questions:

  • Who are the key publics? What is their current opinion on the topic?
  • How important is public opinion for this topic? Will it strongly influence the political agenda?
  • Who are the key interest groups? What are their agendas? How organised and influential are they?
  • Who are the key decision-makers? What drives them—party dynamics, personal preferences, electoral concerns?
  • What political events are coming up that might affect the composition or priorities of the government?

Evidence in the political stream

Many assume that strong evidence will shift political priorities. It rarely does. Political receptivity is shaped by elections, public mood, interest groups, and decision-makers' motivations—not by data. Focus your assessment here on these factors. Evidence becomes valuable later, when a window opens and decision-makers are actively looking for solutions.

Assessing the policy stream

In the policy stream, ideas, proposals, and solutions float in a "primeval soup" where they are tested, revised, and refined until viable options emerge.

Key aspects to assess:

Existing policies: What is the current legal and policy framework? What policies exist at local, national, regional, and international levels?

Policy solutions under discussion: What proposals are being developed or debated? Who is championing them?

Technical feasibility: Are the proposed solutions technically viable? Are there known limitations or gaps?

Political acceptability: How do key actors view the proposed solutions? Are they seen as realistic or controversial?

Probing questions:

  • What is the existing legal framework for this issue at different levels?
  • Are there feasible policy solutions available? Do we need more? Do we need to improve existing ones?
  • Have similar proposals been discussed before? What happened to them?
  • How does the specialised policy community feel about particular solutions?
  • Do we foresee any constraints to the political acceptability of solutions?
  • What evidence gaps exist that might need to be addressed?

Evidence in the policy stream

Evidence is essential for developing and defending solutions. It helps establish technical feasibility, anticipate objections, and identify gaps that opponents might exploit. Having a well-evidenced proposal ready is what allows you to act when a window opens.

2.3 Putting It Together: PFAS Case Study

About this case study

Throughout this guidebook, we follow a team working on PFAS (per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances) regulation to illustrate the concepts and tools in practice. In this chapter, the team assesses their policy context. In Chapter 3, they develop their engagement strategy. In Chapter 4, they design their approach to measuring progress.

The team came to policy engagement through the Conservation Standards—but the methods they use apply regardless of your starting point.

This section shows how the team applied the Policy Context Tool to understand the political landscape around PFAS regulation.

The issue and entry point

PFAS (per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances) are synthetic chemicals that persist in the environment and accumulate in living organisms. They are found in drinking water, food, and everyday products.

This particular team arrived at policy engagement through a Conservation Standards situation analysis. As part of Step 1 ('Assess'), they developed a situation model—a visual representation mapping the causal chain from contributing factors through direct threats to impacts on biodiversity and human wellbeing (Figure 2.1).

The situation model revealed that "inadequate regulatory framework" was a key contributing factor driving PFAS contamination. But identifying this gap was only the first step. To address it, the team needed to understand the political context: What would it actually take to strengthen PFAS regulation?

Figure 2.1: Situation model for PFAS contamination. The model identifies "inadequate regulatory framework" as a contributing factor—the starting point for this team's policy context assessment.

What the situation model showed

Focal values include freshwater ecosystems, soil biodiversity, and coastal/marine ecosystems, as well as human wellbeing targets such as safe drinking water, food safety, and economic livelihoods.

Direct threats are all forms of PFAS pollution (IUCN-CMP Category 9.1: Water-Borne Pollution):

  • Industrial PFAS discharge from manufacturing facilities
  • Application of contaminated biosolids on agricultural land
  • Use of PFAS firefighting foams at military bases and airports

Biophysical factors show how these threats affect ecosystems: surface water contamination, soil contamination, groundwater contamination, and bioaccumulation through food chains.

Contributing factors reveal the drivers behind these threats—and this is where policy becomes visible:

  • Inadequate regulatory framework: Only 12 of approximately 10,000 PFAS compounds are currently regulated
  • Weak sludge regulations: Inadequate controls on biosolids application
  • Polluter-pays principle not enforced: Costs fall on the public and water utilities (estimated €18 billion annually)
  • Chemical industry influence: Lobbying against strict regulations

The situation model also identifies opportunities: a restriction proposal is under development at EU level, new drinking water limits are coming in 2026, public support for a ban exceeds 75%, and water utilities are well organised as advocates.

Applying the Policy Context Tool

With the situation model complete, the team turns to the Policy Context Tool. Their goal is to reduce PFAS threats to biodiversity and human wellbeing—but to do that, they need to understand the political landscape.

Problems stream assessment

The problems stream shows strong recognition of the issue:

Factor Assessment
Focusing events Multiple: health impacts documented, €18 billion annual water treatment costs, contamination studies, cross-border concerns, agricultural impacts, risks to tourism in coastal areas
Indicators TFA in rainwater increased 20-fold in 20 years; PFAS widespread in drinking water and soils; PFAS detected in blood of nearly all citizens
Media attention Very high; multiple stories on health risks and contamination
Problem framing Framed as public health crisis (cancer, immune system effects, reproductive health), economic burden, and environmental contamination

Assessment: The problem is well-recognised. Multiple focusing events keep it on the agenda. The framing as a health and economic issue (not just environmental) broadens the coalition of concerned actors and resonates with different political constituencies.

Political stream assessment

The political stream shows a contested but favourable environment:

Factor Assessment
Public opinion 75%+ support an EU-wide ban; high awareness of health risks; local communities concerned about drinking water contamination
Interest groups - supportive Water utilities (high influence), environmental organisations (medium-high), tourism industry (medium), affected agricultural communities
Interest groups - opposing Chemical industry (very high influence); actively lobbying to reframe regulation as threat to "essential uses"
Government position Current government hesitant due to industry ties; incoming conservative government expected to be receptive to both economic arguments and constituent concerns about health
Political events National elections upcoming; government transition expected

Assessment: Public support is strong. A coalition of supportive interest groups exists, united by economic and health concerns. The incoming conservative government is likely attentive to constituents in electoral districts worried about polluted drinking water. However, the chemical industry has significant influence and is actively working to shape the new government's position. The transition creates a political window—but also a risk that industry framing takes hold first.

Policy stream assessment

The policy stream shows solutions are available but not fully developed:

Factor Assessment
EU framework Restriction proposal under assessment at EU level, including essential/non-essential use distinction; new drinking water limits coming in 2026
National role Member state support needed for EU process; national position will influence outcome
Industry counter-strategy Chemical lobby reframing at national level: arguing certain uses are so essential that any regulation is dangerous
Political acceptability Essential/non-essential distinction provides a balanced middle ground—if industry reframing is countered

Assessment: A viable policy solution already exists at EU level. The team's task is not to develop a new proposal but to ensure national support for the ongoing EU process. The main risk is that industry successfully reframes the debate before the new government forms its position.

Identifying the window and what to couple

A political window is opening with the expected government transition. To make use of it, the team needs to couple three things:

  • The problem: PFAS contamination as a public health and economic crisis—affecting citizens' health, burdening water utilities, threatening tourism and agriculture.
  • The political receptivity: The incoming conservative government is likely receptive to economic arguments from affected industries and to constituent concerns about drinking water and health in their electoral districts.
  • The solution: National support for the EU restriction proposal, which provides a balanced approach through the essential/non-essential use distinction.

The window is predictable (election dates are known) but time-limited (new governments are most receptive in their early months). The main strategic task is to make this coupling before industry reframing takes hold. If the new administration adopts the "essential uses are threatened" narrative first, it could weaken member state support for the EU process.

Chapter 3 shows how the team translates this assessment into a concrete engagement strategy.

2.4 Supporting Tools

The Policy Context Tool provides a high-level framework for assessment. Depending on your needs, you may want to use additional tools for more detailed analysis of specific components. Below is a selection of tools that practitioners have found useful.

Horizon scanning

Useful for: Identifying upcoming focusing events, anticipated political changes, and emerging policy ideas—informing all three streams.

Horizon scanning identifies emerging problems and opportunities at an early stage, allowing timely preparation of strategies. Through structured methods—trend analysis, expert consultation, literature review, and monitoring of scientific and policy signals—teams can detect early indicators of issues that may gain relevance over time.

For conservation teams, this might mean regularly reviewing outputs from larger horizon scanning exercises (such as the annual scan published by Sutherland et al. in Trends in Ecology & Evolution), monitoring policy calendars for scheduled reviews and decisions, and tracking political developments that could open or close windows.

Policymaker ratings

Useful for: Assessing the political stream—mapping decision-maker positions and tracking changes over time.

The policymaker rating method measures political support for a specific advocacy issue among a defined group of policymakers. It uses advocates' existing knowledge to rate policymakers on three scales:

  • Level of support: The policymaker's backing of the issue, based on public behaviour or actions
  • Level of influence: The policymaker's ability to affect the issue
  • Rater confidence: Confidence in the accuracy of the ratings

Ratings can be aggregated across policymakers and combined with contextual data (party affiliation, committee membership) for deeper analysis. The exercise can be repeated over time to track changes in political support.

Legal framework analysis

Useful for: Assessing the policy stream—understanding what solutions exist and where gaps remain.

A legal framework analysis maps the laws, regulations, policies, and international commitments that govern the issue you aim to influence. Understanding the existing framework helps identify what solutions are already in place and where improvements are needed.

Useful databases include:

Environmental law databases:

  • ECOLEX (ecolex.org): Information on environmental treaties, soft-law, national legislation, and judicial decisions. Operated jointly by FAO, IUCN, and UNEP.
  • InforMEA (informea.org): UN portal on Multilateral Environmental Agreements, including national reports, plans, and COP decisions.

Issue-specific databases:

  • FAOLEX (faolex.fao.org): National laws, regulations, and policies on food, agriculture, and natural resource management.
  • CITESlex: Legislation and policies relevant to the implementation of CITES.

Regional databases:

  • EUR-Lex (eur-lex.europa.eu): European Union law, including treaties, legislation, case law, and preparatory documents.
  • AfricanLII (africanlii.org): African regional and national laws.

Evidence needs analysis

Useful for: Assessing the policy stream—determining whether the evidence base for proposed solutions is sufficient.

An evidence needs analysis identifies what scientific data, research findings, or technical information already exists on a topic, and where gaps remain. This helps determine whether targeted research is needed to support a policy proposal.

Approaches include structured literature reviews, gap mapping exercises, and participatory methods that bring together policymakers, practitioners, and researchers to identify priority information needs.

Reference: [TO VERIFY: Sutherland, W.J. et al. (2011). Methods for collaboratively identifying research priorities and emerging issues in science and policy. Methods in Ecology and Evolution, 2(3), 238–247.]
